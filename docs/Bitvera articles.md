### 

### **Layer 2 Explained in Simple Terms**

At its core, Layer 2 is designed to improve how blockchains handle activity.  
Instead of processing every transaction directly on the main blockchain (often referred to as Layer 1), Layer 2 introduces an additional processing layer. This secondary layer handles a portion of transactions separately and then finalizes them on the main network.

A simple way to understand this is to imagine the blockchain as a main highway. When traffic increases, movement slows down. Layer 2 acts as an additional route that allows transactions to move faster without overloading the main system.

The result is:

* faster transaction processing  
* lower fees  
* reduced congestion on the primary blockchain

### 

### **Layer 2 in the Context of Ethereum**

The role of Layer 2 becomes particularly relevant when looking at Ethereum.  
Ethereum supports a large ecosystem of applications, smart contracts, and digital assets. As activity on the network has increased over time, so has the pressure on its infrastructure. High demand often leads to slower confirmations and higher transaction costs.

Layer 2 solutions address this by handling part of the transaction load outside the main Ethereum network. Once processed, the results are recorded back on the blockchain, maintaining the same level of security and verification.

This approach allows Ethereum to scale without compromising its core principles — decentralization and security.

### 

### **Impact on Crypto Exchange Experience**

For users, transaction efficiency is not just a technical detail — it directly affects usability.

A crypto exchange must provide:

* predictable execution  
* reasonable transaction costs  
* consistent performance

Layer 2 contributes to all three. By reducing congestion and optimizing how transactions are processed, it supports a smoother and more reliable experience.

At Bitvera, infrastructure is continuously optimized to align with these developments, ensuring that users benefit from improvements in blockchain performance without added complexity.

### 

### **Why Layer 2 Matters for the Future**

As adoption of cryptocurrencies increases, blockchain systems must handle higher volumes of activity.

Layer 2 plays a critical role in this transition by:

* distributing transaction load more efficiently  
* enabling faster interaction with blockchain-based systems  
* supporting advanced use cases such as decentralized applications and smart contracts

Rather than replacing the main blockchain, Layer 2 enhances it — allowing the ecosystem to scale while maintaining security and structural integrity.

This structure allows users to exchange value directly, without requiring intermediaries such as banks or payment providers.

Transparency is a defining feature. Transaction data is shared across the network, making it visible and verifiable, while cryptographic protection ensures that the information remains secure.

### 

### **How Blockchain Works in Practice**

Blockchain operates through a structured process where transactions are verified and permanently recorded.

When a transaction is initiated, it is broadcast to a network of computers (nodes). These nodes validate the transaction using predefined rules and cryptographic methods. Once verified, the transaction is grouped with others into a “block.”

Each new block is linked to the previous one, creating a chronological chain of records. After confirmation, the data becomes part of the ledger and cannot be easily altered.  
This process removes the need for a central authority and reduces the risk of a single point of failure, as every participant maintains a copy of the ledger.

### 

### **Example: A Crypto Transaction Flow**

In a typical cryptocurrency transaction, the process follows a structured sequence:

* A transaction request is created and sent to the network  
* The transaction details are grouped into a new block  
* Network participants validate the transaction  
* Once approved, the block is added to the existing chain  
* The updated ledger is distributed across the network  
* The transaction is finalized and permanently recorded

All of these steps occur automatically in the background, without requiring user interaction with the underlying mechanics.

### 

### **Security and Data Integrity**

One of the key strengths of blockchain is its resistance to modification.  
Once a transaction is recorded and confirmed, altering it would require changing data across the majority of the network simultaneously, which is highly impractical. This makes blockchain particularly reliable for recording financial transactions.  
The decentralized structure also enhances resilience. Since data is not stored in a single location, the system remains operational even if part of the network is disrupted.

### 

### **Beyond Crypto: Broader Applications**

Although blockchain is most commonly associated with cryptocurrencies, its use extends beyond digital assets.

The same structure can be applied to:

* supply chain tracking  
* secure data sharing  
* identity verification  
* healthcare record management  
* cybersecurity systems

In addition, blockchain supports emerging technologies such as decentralized finance (DeFi), digital assets, and other distributed applications.

### 

### **Efficiency and Operational Benefits**

Blockchain systems operate continuously, without dependency on centralized schedules or intermediaries.

This enables:

* faster transaction processing  
* reduced reliance on third parties  
* lower operational overhead in certain use cases

By streamlining how data is recorded and verified, blockchain introduces a more direct and efficient method of handling transactions.

# Hot Wallet or Cold Wallet: How to Store Cryptocurrency the Right Way

***This article and its content have been produced and disseminated to persons outside of the United Kingdom. The information provided is not directed at or intended for distribution to, or use by, any person or entity in the UK. The financial products and services mentioned in this article are not eligible for the UK. When it comes to cryptocurrency, storage is not just a technical detail — it defines your level of control over your assets. Unlike traditional finance, there is no bank, no recovery service, and no fallback if something goes wrong. The way your wallet is set up directly determines how exposed or protected your funds are.

The distinction between hot and cold wallets is often presented as a simple choice, but in reality, it reflects a deeper difference between accessibility and security.

### 

### **Understanding What a Wallet Actually Does**

A common misconception is that a wallet “stores” cryptocurrency. In practice, assets remain on the blockchain, while the wallet holds the private keys that grant access to them.

This means the real question is not where your crypto is stored, but where your **access rights** are stored and how exposed they are. Once this is clear, the difference between hot and cold wallets becomes much more logical.

### 

### **Cold Wallets: Maximum Control Through Isolation**

Cold wallets are defined by one key principle — they remain offline. This isolation removes the primary attack surface used in most crypto theft cases: internet exposure.

Hardware wallets, offline computers, and even paper-based storage fall into this category. In all cases, private keys are generated and stored in an environment that does not interact with online systems during normal operation.

Because of this, remote attacks become extremely difficult. There is no direct way to access the wallet without physical possession of the device or the recovery credentials. This makes cold storage the standard choice for holding larger amounts of cryptocurrency over longer periods.

However, this level of protection comes with operational friction. Every transaction requires deliberate action: connecting the device, confirming details, and manually authorizing the transfer. It is not designed for speed — it is designed for control.

There is also a different type of risk involved. If access credentials are lost, damaged, or improperly stored, recovery may be impossible. Cold wallets protect against external threats, but they demand discipline from the user.

### 

### **Hot Wallets: Accessibility and Speed**

Hot wallets operate oppositely. They are connected to the internet, which allows for immediate access to funds and fast transaction execution.

These include mobile apps, desktop wallets, and browser-based solutions. Their primary advantage is usability. Transactions can be initiated within seconds, making them practical for frequent activities such as exchanging or everyday transfers.

This convenience is the reason hot wallets are widely used, but it also defines their limitations. Being online means they are exposed to risks such as phishing, malware, unauthorized access, and compromised devices.

In practice, this does not make hot wallets unsafe by default — it means their safety depends heavily on user behavior. Poor password management, unsecured devices, or interaction with untrusted sources can significantly increase risk.

For this reason, hot wallets are generally used for smaller, active balances rather than long-term storage.

### 

### **The Real Difference: Exposure vs Control**

The distinction between hot and cold wallets is not about which one is “better.” It is about how much exposure you are willing to accept in exchange for convenience.

Cold wallets reduce exposure to near zero, but require effort to use.

Hot wallets maximize accessibility but increase the number of possible attack vectors.  
Understanding this is more important than the technical differences between wallet types.

### 

### **Practical Approach: Combining Both**

In practice, most users do not rely on a single wallet type.

A common approach is to separate funds based on purpose. Assets that are not intended for regular use are kept in cold storage, where they remain protected from online risks. At the same time, a smaller portion is maintained in a hot wallet to allow for quick transactions and operational flexibility.

This structure mirrors how value is managed in other systems — not all funds are kept in immediate circulation, and not all are locked away.

### 

### **Final Perspective**

Crypto security is not achieved by choosing a specific wallet, but by understanding how exposure works.

Hot wallets provide speed and convenience, making them suitable for active use. Cold wallets provide isolation and control, making them suitable for long-term storage. The effective approach is not to choose between them, but to use each where it makes sense.

In a system where transactions are irreversible and ownership is absolute, the way access is managed becomes the foundation of security.

## 

The terms “coin” and “token” are often used interchangeably, which creates confusion, especially for those entering the crypto space. While both represent digital value and can be transferred or exchanged, they are not the same in structure or purpose.

The difference is not about price or popularity — it is about **how the asset is built and what role it plays within a blockchain system**.

### 

### **What Defines a Coin**

A coin is a cryptocurrency that operates on its **own blockchain**.

Bitcoin is the clearest example. It runs on the Bitcoin network, and every transaction involving Bitcoin is recorded directly on that blockchain. The same applies to other coins like Ethereum, Litecoin, or similar assets — each exists as a native unit of its own system.

Coins are typically designed to function as:

* a medium of exchange  
* a store of value  
* a unit used to pay transaction fees within their network

Because they are native to their blockchain, coins are fundamental to how that network operates. Without them, the system itself would not function.

### 

### **What Defines a Token**

A token does not have its own blockchain. Instead, it is created **on top of an existing blockchain**.

For example, many tokens are built on the Ethereum network. They rely on Ethereum’s infrastructure for security, transaction processing, and record-keeping.

Tokens are generally used for more specific purposes. They can represent:

* access to a service  
* participation in a platform  
* digital assets within applications  
* stable value tied to external references (such as stablecoins)

Because tokens are built using existing frameworks, they are easier to create and deploy compared to launching a new blockchain.

### 

### **Structural Difference in Practice**

The key distinction is architectural.

A coin is part of the base layer — it powers the blockchain itself.

A token exists on top of that layer — it uses the blockchain but does not define it.

When a transaction occurs, nothing physically “moves.” Instead, the blockchain updates balances and records the change. This applies to both coins and tokens, but the difference lies in where that record is maintained.

Coins are recorded on their own networks.

Tokens are recorded on the networks they are built on.

### 

### **Use Cases and Practical Meaning**

Coins are typically used when the objective is **the transfer of value or network operation**.  
Tokens are used when the objective is tied to a **specific function within an ecosystem** — such as accessing a platform, interacting with an application, or representing a digital asset.

This is why, in practice:

* coins are often associated with payments and transfers  
* tokens are often associated with services, platforms, or utilities

However, the distinction is not always visible at the surface level, which is why confusion persists.

### 

### **Final Overview**

The difference between a coin and a token is structural rather than conceptual.  
Coins operate on their own blockchains and form the foundation of those networks. Tokens are built on existing blockchains and serve specific purposes within broader ecosystems.  
Understanding this distinction helps clarify how different digital assets function and why they exist within the crypto market.
